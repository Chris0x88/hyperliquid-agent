# Hormuz Blockade: Benchmark Deliverability, SPR Grades, and Market Structure Research

**Date:** 2026-04-02
**Context:** Strait of Hormuz effectively closed since Feb 28, 2026 following US-Israeli strikes on Iran

---

## 1. Dubai/Oman Benchmark Deliverable Grades

### The Five Deliverable Grades (Pre-Crisis)

The Platts Dubai benchmark allows delivery of five crude grades, at the seller's option:

| Grade | Origin | Production (pre-war) | Loads From | Requires Hormuz Transit? |
|-------|--------|---------------------|------------|--------------------------|
| **Dubai** | UAE (Dubai) | ~50,000 b/d | Fateh terminal (Persian Gulf) | YES |
| **Oman Export Blend** | Oman | ~950,000 b/d | Mina al Fahl (Gulf of Oman) | NO |
| **Upper Zakum** | UAE (Abu Dhabi offshore) | ~1,000,000+ b/d | Zirku Island / Jebel Dhanna (Persian Gulf) | YES |
| **Al Shaheen** | Qatar | ~300,000 b/d | Ras Laffan / Halul Island (Persian Gulf) | YES |
| **Murban** | UAE (Abu Dhabi onshore) | ~1,600,000 b/d | Jebel Dhanna (Gulf) OR Fujairah (Gulf of Oman via ADCOP pipeline) | SPLIT -- Gulf loadings YES, Fujairah NO |

**Total deliverable production: ~3.5+ million b/d**

### Assessment Process

- Assessed daily by S&P Global (Platts) via the Market on Close (MOC) process at 16:30 Singapore time
- Buyers accept delivery of Dubai itself or alternative delivery of any of the four other grades
- The benchmark prices approximately 18 million b/d of crude globally (mostly Asian-bound Middle East crude)

## 2. Percentage of Dubai Deliverables Locked Behind Hormuz

### What Platts Has Done

**March 2, 2026:** Platts suspended nominations of crude grades requiring Strait of Hormuz transit. This excluded:
- Dubai crude (~50,000 b/d) -- LOCKED
- Upper Zakum (~1,000,000+ b/d) -- LOCKED
- Al Shaheen (~300,000 b/d) -- LOCKED
- Murban loading from Jebel Dhanna -- LOCKED

**Only two delivery options remain:**
1. **Oman** loading from Mina al Fahl (~950,000 b/d) -- outside Hormuz
2. **Murban** loading from Fujairah (~1,500,000-1,800,000 b/d pipeline capacity) -- outside Hormuz via ADCOP pipeline

### Volume Math

- **Pre-crisis deliverable production:** ~3,500,000+ b/d across all five grades
- **Grades requiring Hormuz transit:** Dubai + Upper Zakum + Al Shaheen + Gulf-loaded Murban = ~1,350,000+ b/d of the original deliverable pool PLUS loss of the primary Murban loading terminal
- **Platts stated the cut reduced deliverable crude by ~40-70%** (sources vary; initial reports said 40%, later assessments said up to 70% when accounting for actual shut-in production)

### But It Gets Worse -- Physical Disruptions to the "Safe" Routes

Even the non-Hormuz routes are now compromised:

- **Fujairah (Murban's bypass route):** Hit by drone strikes on March 14 and March 16, 2026. Oil loading operations suspended multiple times. A third attack in four days hit on ~March 18. Operations have resumed intermittently but remain at risk.
- **Mina al Fahl (Oman):** Oman ordered all vessels to leave the terminal after security risks escalated. Drone strikes hit Omani energy facilities on ~March 12.
- **UAE production shut-ins:** UAE crude output fell by MORE THAN HALF. ADNOC cut production to ~1.5 million b/d (from 3.4 million b/d in January). ALL offshore production went offline. Upper Zakum, Das Blend, Umm Lulu -- all shut in.

### Net Assessment

The Dubai benchmark is operating on a fraction of its normal deliverable base. With only Murban-via-Fujairah and Oman theoretically available, and both of those routes under periodic attack, **the benchmark is under existential stress**. Platts has been forced into emergency methodology changes (suspending Murban quality adjustments on March 20) to keep the benchmark functional.

## 3. Brent-Dubai EFS (Exchange of Futures for Swaps)

### How the EFS Works

The Brent-Dubai EFS is the spread between ICE Brent futures and Dubai swaps. It is the critical pricing link between:
- The Atlantic Basin (Brent-priced crude: North Sea, West Africa, some US exports)
- The Middle East/Asia (Dubai-priced crude: Persian Gulf producers, Asian buyers)

**Normal behavior:** Brent trades at a premium to Dubai (positive EFS) because Brent is lighter/sweeter. Typical range: $1-3/bbl.

**The EFS drives arbitrage:** When the EFS widens, it becomes economic to ship Atlantic Basin crude to Asia. When it narrows, Atlantic barrels stay west.

### What Happened in 2026

**Pre-crisis:** EFS was under $2/bbl.

**Early March 2026:** EFS surged above $6/bbl -- the widest gap in years. This was the initial reaction as traders priced in Hormuz closure risk to the Dubai side.

**Then the EFS INVERTED:** By late March, Dubai crude was trading at a RECORD PREMIUM to Brent. The traditional Brent>Dubai relationship flipped entirely.

- Saudi Aramco set its April Arab Light OSP at $2.50/bbl above Oman-Dubai average (massive increase)
- Cash differentials for Middle East grades hit all-time highs
- Dubai crude was reported trading 20%+ higher than Brent

### Why the Inversion Matters

This is extraordinary and signals the sour crude market has completely decoupled from the sweet market:

1. **Sour crude scarcity:** The ~10 million b/d locked behind Hormuz is overwhelmingly medium/heavy sour crude. Asian refineries are configured for sour crude (Japan/Taiwan ~60% sour-dependent). They cannot easily substitute sweet barrels.
2. **Sweet crude surplus:** SPR releases (mostly sweet) and Atlantic Basin barrels flood the WTI/Brent complex, depressing sweet crude prices relative to sour.
3. **Physical undeliverability premium:** Buyers paying any price for the few sour barrels that can actually be physically delivered.

## 4. SPR Crude Grades

### Storage Site Specifications

| Site | Location | Capacity | Status |
|------|----------|----------|--------|
| **Bryan Mound** | Brazoria County, TX (near Freeport) | 247.1 MMbbl, 19 caverns | Operational since 1986 |
| **Big Hill** | Jefferson County, TX (near Beaumont) | 170.0 MMbbl, 14 caverns | Operational since 1991 |
| **West Hackberry** | Cameron Parish, LA (near Lake Charles) | ~160 MMbbl | Operational |
| **Bayou Choctaw** | Iberville Parish, LA | 76.0 MMbbl, 6 caverns | Operational |

### Crude Quality

**Overall mix:** ~60% sour, ~40% sweet

- **Sweet crude:** <0.50% sulfur content
- **Sour crude:** 0.50% - 1.99% sulfur content
- Crude is commingled by quality within caverns -- sweet and sour streams are kept separate
- **West Hackberry Sweet stream:** 37 API gravity, 0.29% sulfur (light sweet -- close to WTI quality)
- Other streams vary; DOE maintains detailed assay data per cavern

### Pre-Release Inventory

As of December 31, 2025: **411 million barrels** (equivalent to ~125 days of US crude net imports)

### How SPR Releases Affect Pricing

**SPR crude is fundamentally a WTI-complex product:**
- All four sites are on the US Gulf Coast
- Connected via pipeline to Houston, Texas City, Freeport, Beaumont-Port Arthur, Lake Charles, New Orleans refinery hubs
- Released into the same delivery infrastructure as WTI (Cushing-connected)
- SPR releases directly increase Gulf Coast crude supply, pressuring WTI and the WTI-Brent spread

**The grade mismatch problem:**
- SPR is 60% sour -- but the crisis is a SOUR crude crisis
- SPR sour grades are lighter than the medium-heavy sour Gulf crudes that Asian refiners need
- SPR sweet crude (40%) adds to an already-oversupplied sweet market
- This means SPR releases are more effective at suppressing WTI/Brent than at solving the actual physical shortage of Gulf sour grades

## 5. IEA Coordinated Release Mechanics

### The 400 Million Barrel Release

**Announced:** March 11, 2026
**Scale:** Largest coordinated release in IEA's 52-year history
**Participants:** All 32 IEA member countries unanimously agreed

### Country Contributions

| Country | Volume | Notes |
|---------|--------|-------|
| **United States** | 172 million bbl | From SPR; structured as exchange (loan with interest); 1.4 million b/d over 120 days |
| **Japan** | ~80 million bbl | 15-day release from company storage + 1-month from government strategic reserves |
| **South Korea** | 22.46 million bbl | From strategic reserves |
| **United Kingdom** | 13.5 million bbl | |
| **Spain** | ~11.5 million bbl | Over 90 days |
| **Germany** | Contributing (volume TBD) | Holds 110M bbl crude + 67M bbl products |
| **France** | Contributing (volume TBD) | Holds ~120M bbl total; ~30% crude, 50% gasoil, 9% gasoline, 7.8% jet fuel |
| **Canada** | Major contributor | |
| **Austria** | Contributing | |

**Release timeline:**
- Asia-Oceania stocks: Released immediately (starting ~March 12-15)
- Americas and Europe stocks: Released from end of March

### How Different Reserve Types Affect Different Benchmarks

**US SPR (WTI-linked):**
- 172 million barrels of Gulf Coast crude
- Directly suppresses WTI pricing
- Widens the WTI discount to Brent
- 1.4 million b/d rate = only 15% of the ~10 million b/d lost from Hormuz

**European reserves (Brent-linked/product stocks):**
- European strategic stocks are a MIX of crude and refined products
- France: 30% crude, 70% products (gasoil, gasoline, jet fuel, heating oil)
- Germany: mix of crude and finished products
- European crude releases flow into the Brent/BFOET pricing complex
- Product releases directly address refined product shortages without flowing through crude benchmarks
- Net effect: moderate downward pressure on Brent, direct relief on European diesel/gasoline

**Japanese/Korean reserves (Dubai-linked demand):**
- Japan and Korea are the biggest Asian strategic stockholders
- Their refineries are configured for medium sour crude (~60% of Japanese imports are Middle Eastern sour)
- Japanese strategic crude stocks likely include sour grades purchased from Gulf producers
- These releases provide DIRECT relief to the Dubai-priced market
- However, volumes are small relative to the disruption

### The Fundamental Inadequacy

400 million barrels / 10 million b/d disruption = **40 days of coverage**

Even at peak release rates, the coordinated IEA action replaces only a fraction of lost supply. If Hormuz remains closed, strategic stocks will be exhausted within months.

## 6. Asian Crude Pricing in Crisis

### Normal State

- Most Asian crude is priced off Dubai/Oman (not Brent)
- Saudi Aramco's OSP for Asia = premium/discount to Oman-Dubai average
- ~18 million b/d of crude globally priced off the Dubai benchmark
- Japanese, Korean, Chinese, Indian, Taiwanese refineries all configured for medium sour Gulf crude

### What Is Happening Now

**The Dubai benchmark is functionally impaired:**
- Three of five deliverable grades are locked behind Hormuz
- The remaining two (Oman, Fujairah-Murban) are under periodic drone attack
- Some market participants have told Reuters the benchmark is "effectively broken"
- Many have stopped dealing with Dubai-priced cargoes or trading Dubai derivatives

**Asian buyers are switching to Brent-priced and WTI-priced alternatives:**
- Japanese refiner Taiyo Oil bought 2 million barrels of US light crude priced off ICE Brent for July delivery at ~$19/bbl above Brent
- Multiple Japanese buyers have secured US cargoes priced off Brent
- This marks a clear break from the traditional Dubai-linked pricing system
- South Korean and Indian buyers are reportedly doing the same

**The grade substitution problem:**
- Asian refinery configurations are optimized for medium sour crude
- US exports and Atlantic Basin crude are predominantly light sweet
- Light sweet crude produces different product slates (more gasoline/naphtha, less diesel/fuel oil)
- Asian refineries taking light sweet crude run at reduced efficiency
- Some complex Asian refineries can handle the switch; simple hydro-skimming refineries cannot

**Saudi Arabia's response:**
- April Arab Light OSP set at $2.50/bbl above Oman-Dubai average (massive premium increase)
- Saudi Arabia still prices off Oman-Dubai but is capturing the extreme tightness
- Saudi crude that CAN reach Asian buyers (via Red Sea/Suez or overland) commands massive premiums

## 7. Benchmark Stress/Failure Risk

### Has a Benchmark Lost Deliverability Before?

**Brent's chronic deliverability problem (ongoing since 1990s):**
- Original Brent crude production has declined from 500,000+ b/d to near zero
- Platts/ICE solved this by repeatedly adding grades: Forties (2002), Oseberg, Ekofisk, Troll, and most recently WTI Midland (2023)
- BFOET constitutes only ~1% of world crude production
- The benchmark has survived through creative expansion, never through a sudden physical disruption

**Key difference with Dubai 2026:** Brent's problem was GRADUAL depletion solved incrementally. Dubai's problem is SUDDEN physical inaccessibility of 40-70% of deliverable supply with no ready substitutes that are sour and Gulf-located.

### Is the Dubai Benchmark Breaking?

Evidence it is under extreme stress:
1. Platts excluded 3 of 5 grades on March 2
2. Emergency methodology changes on March 20 (Murban quality adjustment suspended)
3. Record MOC trading volumes in March (speculators and hedgers scrambling)
4. Market participants publicly calling the benchmark "effectively broken"
5. Dubai trading at 20%+ premium to Brent (unprecedented inversion)
6. Asian buyers abandoning Dubai pricing for Brent-based contracts
7. Physical delivery routes (Fujairah, Mina al Fahl) under intermittent attack

### Consequences of Benchmark Failure

If Dubai effectively breaks:

1. **Pricing chaos for ~18 million b/d:** Every Middle Eastern OSP contract references Dubai/Oman. Saudi Aramco, Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar -- all use Dubai/Oman as their Asian pricing base.
2. **Spread dislocation:** The EFS becomes meaningless. The link between Atlantic and Pacific crude markets breaks.
3. **Refinery margin uncertainty:** Asian refiners cannot calculate input costs, making purchasing decisions nearly impossible.
4. **Potential shift to Murban or alternative benchmark:** ADNOC's ICE Murban Futures (listed on ICE Futures Abu Dhabi) could fill the gap -- Murban is deliverable via Fujairah, outside Hormuz. But Fujairah is being attacked.
5. **WTI as global benchmark?** US crude exports are the only large, accessible sweet crude supply. Asian buyers are already pricing off Brent. If this persists, WTI/Brent could become the de facto global benchmark with Dubai relegated.
6. **Long-term structural shift:** Even after Hormuz reopens, buyers may permanently diversify away from Dubai-priced contracts, accelerating the benchmark's decline.

## Trading Implications

1. **BRENTOIL (HL perp tracks Brent):** Brent is being SUPPRESSED relative to sour grades by SPR sweet crude floods and Atlantic Basin oversupply. The real crisis is in sour/Dubai, not Brent. Brent is elevated ($100+) but structurally capped by SPR releases.

2. **The sour premium is the real trade:** Dubai/sour crude is where the physical shortage exists. Brent is a derivative beneficiary, not the epicenter.

3. **SPR exhaustion timeline:** 400 million barrels / ~10 million b/d gap = 40 days. If Hormuz stays closed past mid-April, strategic reserves start running low. Second-order price spike incoming.

4. **Benchmark structural shift risk:** If Dubai permanently impairs, the entire Middle East pricing architecture changes. This is a generational market structure event.

5. **Watch Fujairah and Mina al Fahl:** These are the last two physical delivery points keeping the Dubai benchmark alive. If either is knocked offline for an extended period, the benchmark may formally suspend.
