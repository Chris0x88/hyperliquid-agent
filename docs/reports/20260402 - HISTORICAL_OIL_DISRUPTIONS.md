# Historical Oil Supply Disruptions: Comprehensive Comparison
## Research Date: 2026-04-02

---

## 1. 1973 Arab Oil Embargo (Oct 1973 - Mar 1974)

**Trigger:** OAPEC embargo on US and Netherlands after Yom Kippur War support for Israel.

**Supply Disrupted:** 4.5 mb/d (~7% of global supply)
- Arab producers ordered immediate 5% production cut, followed by additional 5% monthly reductions
- Exports to the West down 60-70% by November 1973

**Price Impact:**
- Pre-crisis: ~$2.90/bbl
- Peak: $11.65/bbl (Jan 1974)
- Change: +302% (nearly quadrupled)

**Duration:** ~5 months (Oct 17, 1973 to Mar 1974)

**Economic Impact:**
- US GDP: +5.7% (1973) -> -0.5% (1974); overall ~2.5-4.7% GDP decline
- US inflation: 3.4% (1972) -> 12.3% (1974)
- US unemployment: 4.6% (Oct 1973) -> 9.0% (May 1975)
- Japan GDP: +8% (1973) -> -1.2% (1974); ~7% decline
- UK GDP: +7.3% (1973) -> -1.7% (1974); recession lasted 1973-1975
- Triggered severe 1973-1975 recession and coined the term "stagflation"

**Key Detail:** No strategic reserve system existed. IEA was founded in 1974 as a direct response.

## 2. 1979 Iranian Revolution (Late 1978 - 1980)

**Trigger:** Political revolution overthrew the Shah; oil workers went on strike.

**Supply Disrupted:** 4.8 mb/d peak loss (~7% of world production)
- Iran's output collapsed by January 1979
- Saudi Arabia and OPEC partially offset; net global loss ~4%
- Average drop of 3.9 mb/d over the 1978-1981 period

**Price Impact:**
- Pre-crisis: ~$13/bbl (mid-1979)
- Peak: $34-39.50/bbl (mid-1980)
- Change: +150-200% (more than doubled)

**Duration:** ~18 months of acute disruption

**Economic Impact:**
- US CPI inflation: hit 9% by end of 1979, continued rising
- Triggered the most severe US recession since the Great Depression (until 2008)
- Stagflation: simultaneous high inflation + high unemployment
- World GDP dropped ~3% from trend
- Volcker's rate hikes (1979-1983) eventually broke inflation but pushed unemployment to 10.4% (Feb 1983)

**Key Detail:** The actual physical shortage was modest (~4% net), but panic hoarding and speculative buying amplified the price impact far beyond fundamentals. This is the classic case of fear-driven price overshoot.

## 3. 1980 Iran-Iraq War (Sep 1980 - 1988)

**Trigger:** Iraq invaded Iran; both countries' oil infrastructure targeted.

**Supply Disrupted:** ~3.5-4.0 mb/d combined (further ~7% drop in global production)
- Built on top of already-reduced Iranian output from the revolution
- Iraq production dropped sharply; Iran's remained depressed

**Price Impact:**
- Prices were already elevated from 1979 crisis
- OPEC fixed marker crude at $30/bbl (Sep 17, 1980)
- Relatively modest additional price impact vs. the revolution
- Prices did not return to pre-1979 levels until mid-1980s

**Duration:** War lasted 8 years (1980-1988), but acute oil disruption concentrated in first 2 years

**Economic Impact:**
- Less dramatic than 1979 because:
  - Contingency measures from 1973 lesson already in place
  - Gulf states developed overland shipping routes
  - Global demand was declining
  - Other producers ramped up
- Prices gradually declined through mid-1980s as market adjusted

**Key Detail:** Demonstrates that market adaptation can blunt the impact of even major disruptions if they are gradual and alternatives exist.

## 4. 1990 Gulf War / Kuwait Invasion (Aug 1990 - Feb 1991)

**Trigger:** Iraq invaded Kuwait; UN embargo on both countries' exports.

**Supply Disrupted:** 4.3 mb/d (combined Iraqi + Kuwaiti output)
- Nearly one-third of Gulf exports removed

**Price Impact:**
- Pre-crisis: $17/bbl (Jul 1990)
- Peak: $46/bbl (mid-Oct 1990)
- Change: +170% peak-to-trough
- Average monthly: $17 (Jul) -> $36 (Oct)

**Duration:** ~9 months for the price spike; Kuwaiti oil fires not extinguished until Nov 1991

**Economic Impact:**
- Contributed to the early 1990s US recession
- US GDP: +1.9% (1990) -> -0.17% (1991)
- Less severe and shorter than 1970s crises
- Recovery relatively quick once coalition victory became clear

**Key Detail:** Military success rapidly resolved supply fears. The SPR existed and was deployed for the first time. Saudi spare capacity filled much of the gap.

## 5. 2019 Abqaiq-Khurais Attack (Sep 14, 2019)

**Trigger:** Drone/missile attack on Saudi Aramco processing facilities.

**Supply Disrupted:** 5.7 mb/d + 0.7 mb/d NGL = 6.4 mb/d total
- Largest single-day supply outage in modern oil history (at the time)
- Knocked out ~50% of Saudi production instantly

**Price Impact:**
- Pre-attack: ~$60/bbl
- Post-attack: ~$69/bbl
- Change: +14-15% (single day - largest one-day move in a decade)
- Brent jumped from $60 to $69; WTI from ~$55 to $63

**Duration:** Remarkably short
- 2 mb/d restored within 3 days (by Sep 17)
- Full production capacity restored by end of September (~2 weeks)
- Prices fell BELOW pre-attack levels within weeks

**Economic Impact:** Negligible due to rapid recovery

**Key Detail:** Demonstrated Saudi Aramco's extraordinary infrastructure resilience and spare capacity. Also showed that markets price duration expectations, not just initial magnitude. The market looked through the disruption because it correctly assessed it as temporary.

## 6. 2026 Strait of Hormuz Closure (Late Feb 2026 - Ongoing)

**Trigger:** US-Iran military conflict; Iran attacks civilian ships and energy infrastructure; Strait effectively closed to commercial traffic since ~Mar 2.

**Supply Disrupted:** ~15.8-20 mb/d of oil flows stranded
- Strait normally handles ~20 mb/d (~20% of global seaborne oil trade)
- Net global supply loss estimated at ~8 mb/d after offsets (IEA March report)
- Largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market

**Price Impact:**
- Pre-crisis: $66/bbl (Brent, Feb 28)
- Peak: $126/bbl (Brent peak); $118/bbl sustained
- WTI: crossed $100 for first time since 2022
- Change: ~60% in first week; ~90% peak
- Dubai physical crude: $126/bbl (Mar 27)
- US gasoline: exceeded $5/gal in some states; $8/gal in California

**Duration:** Ongoing as of Apr 2, 2026 (~1 month so far)

**Economic Impact (Projected/Early):**
- Goldman Sachs: $14-18/bbl geopolitical risk premium in current prices
- IEA: Global oil supply projected to plunge 8 mb/d in March
- US inflation already accelerating via gasoline prices
- Developing nations most exposed (Vietnam, Pakistan, Indonesia have <20 days reserves)

**Response:**
- IEA: 400 million barrel coordinated strategic reserve release (largest in 52-year history)
- US contribution: 172.2 million barrels (largest single-country contribution ever)
- US SPR was only 60% full (415 million barrels) going in
- OPEC+: pledged 206,000 bpd increase (token gesture)
- US temporarily lifted sanctions on some Russian and Iranian oil
- 412 million barrels total from 32 countries over 4 months

## 7. THE WTI-BRENT SPREAD QUESTION

**Has WTI ever traded above Brent during a Middle East crisis?**

**Short answer: No.** In every major Middle East supply disruption, Brent (the waterborne, internationally-traded benchmark) has traded at a premium to WTI (the inland US benchmark). This is structural:

- Brent prices the barrels most exposed to seaborne trade disruption
- WTI is inland, US-anchored, less exposed to Strait logistics
- During the 2026 Hormuz crisis, the Brent-WTI spread blew out to $15-18/bbl (Brent at $118, WTI at $102-103)
- This is the widest spread since 2011-2015 (when the shale glut pushed WTI to a $24 discount)

**Historical spread context:**
- Pre-2010: WTI traditionally traded at a $1-2 PREMIUM to Brent (due to higher quality)
- 2011-2015: Spread reversed and widened to $24/bbl (WTI discount) due to Cushing glut
- 2020 COVID: WTI went to -$37.63 while Brent held $18 (storage crisis, not geopolitical)
- 2026 Hormuz: Spread widened to $10-18/bbl (Brent premium), reflecting Middle East physical market stress

**The current situation (Brent >> WTI) is NOT unprecedented in direction, but the magnitude ($15-18) during an active military conflict IS unusual.** It reflects the US's unique position as a net exporter insulated from Hormuz disruption.

## 8. ECONOMIC AFTERMATH PATTERNS

Every major oil shock since 1973 has preceded or caused a US recession:

| Crisis | Recession | Inflation Peak | Unemployment Peak | Recovery Time |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| 1973 Embargo | 1973-75 | 12.3% (1974) | 9.0% (May 1975) | ~3 years |
| 1979 Revolution | 1980-82 | 14.8% (1980) | 10.4% (Feb 1983) | ~4 years |
| 1990 Gulf War | 1990-91 | 6.3% (1990) | 7.8% (Jun 1992) | ~1.5 years |
| 2026 Hormuz | TBD | Accelerating | TBD | TBD |

**The pattern:** Oil shock -> inflation spike -> Fed tightening -> recession -> eventual recovery.

The 1970s were the worst because the shocks were sequential (1973 + 1979) and monetary policy initially accommodated inflation before Volcker's harsh correction. The 1990 shock was milder because it was shorter and the economy had already built in energy efficiency improvements.

## 9. WHY 2026 IS STRUCTURALLY DIFFERENT

### Factors That Make It LESS Damaging (vs. 1970s):
1. **US is now a net oil exporter** (~13.4 mb/d production). In 1973, the US was a net importer with declining domestic production.
2. **Oil's share of primary energy has fallen** from 46.2% (1973) to 30.2% (2026).
3. **Strategic Petroleum Reserves exist** - 400M barrel coordinated release (vs. nothing in 1973).
4. **Shale provides rapid supply response** capability that didn't exist before.
5. **Financial derivatives markets** allow hedging and risk distribution.
6. **Energy efficiency** - GDP per barrel of oil consumed is far higher than in the 1970s.

### Factors That Make It MORE Dangerous:
1. **Scale is unprecedented** - 8-20 mb/d disrupted vs. 4-5 mb/d in all prior crises.
2. **Duration uncertainty** - Military conflicts have unpredictable timelines. 1973 embargo lasted 5 months. This could last much longer.
3. **SPR was only 60% full** going in (415M barrels). After the 172M barrel drawdown, the US will have minimal buffer remaining.
4. **Developing nations have tiny reserves** - Vietnam, Pakistan, Indonesia have <20 days. Social instability risk.
5. **Globalized supply chains** are more complex and interdependent than in the 1970s.
6. **Existing inflation concerns** before the crisis even started.
7. **OPEC+ spare capacity is limited** - the 206K bpd pledge is a drop in the bucket vs. 8+ mb/d loss.
8. **Physical crude markets (Dubai) pricing at $126** suggests the paper market is still underpricing the physical shortage.

### The Net Assessment:
The US is better positioned than in any prior oil crisis due to domestic production. But the REST of the world - particularly Asia, which depends on Hormuz for 80%+ of its oil imports - faces the most severe supply crisis in history. The question is whether global economic damage ricochets back to the US through trade channels, financial markets, and demand destruction.
